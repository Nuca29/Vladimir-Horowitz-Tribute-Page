Vladimir Horowitz

"For me, the intellect is always the guide but not the goal of the performance. Three things have to be coordinated, and not one must stick out. Not too much intellect because it can become scholastic. Not too much heart because it can become schmaltz. Not too much technique because you become a mechanic."

Here's a time line of Vladimir Horowitz's life:

  • 1903 - Born in Kiev, Ukraine
  • 1912 - Entered the Kiev Conservatory
  • 1920 - First solo recital in Kharkov, Ukraine
  • 1925 - Emigrated to Germany, later on December 18, Horowitz made his first appearance outside his home country, in Berlin
  • 1928 - United States debut in Carnegie Hall. Olin Downes, an American music critic, called his playing a "tornado unleashed from the steppes."
  • 1930 - First European-produced recording
  • 1936-39 - First "retirement" from the stage
  • 1933 - Played for the first time with the conductor Arturo Toscanini in a performance of Beethoven's Piano Concerto No. 5
  • 1944 - Became an American citizen
  • 1953-65 - Second "retirement" from the stage
  • 1959 - RCA Victor issued the live 1943 performance of the Tchaikovsky Piano Concerto No. 1 with Horowitz and Toscanini, and it was selected for the Grammy Hall of Fame.
  • 1968 - Made his television debut in a concert taped at Carnegie Hall on February 1
  • 1971-74 - Third "retirement" from the stage
  • 1983-85 - Fourth "retirement" from the stage
  • 1985 - Signed with Deutsche Grammophon and made studio and live recordings until 1989, including his only recording of Mozart's Piano Concerto No. 23
  • 1986 - Was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the highest civilian honor bestowed by the United States, by President Ronald Reagan
  • 1987 - Final tour in Europe
  • 1989 - Died on November 5, in New-York City of a heart attack, at the age of 86.

"This is the way I always dreamed my concerto should be played, but I never expected to hear it that way on Earth."

-- Sergei Rachmaninoff after hearing Horowitz's performance of his 3rd concerto on August 7, 1942

"No matter how difficult and complicated the piece, Horowitz would make it sound easy. And above all there were his stupendous fortissimos - that orchestral body of tone that only Horowitz could produce."

-- Harold C. Schonberg, music critic for The New York Times and the author of "The Great Pianists"
